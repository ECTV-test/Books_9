[[CHAPTER: Chapter 1]]

It's true that men and women have sometimes fallen in love at first sight. 
This kind of love is risky, especially before she sees him in a business suit or he sees her without makeup. 
But it does happen, and this story is about one such instance—though thankfully, it won't overshadow more important topics like drinks, policemen, horses, and earldoms.

During a certain war, a group called the Gentle Riders made a name for themselves. 
They were a mix of wild men from the West and the aristocracy from the East. 
In their uniforms, you couldn't tell them apart, so they became good friends.

Ellsworth Remsen, who came from an old New York family and had a modest ten million dollars, enjoyed the adventure with the Gentle Riders. 
The war was a big adventure for him, so he didn't miss his usual games and meals much.

One of the troopers was a friendly, calm young man named O’Roon. 
Remsen liked him a lot. 
They rode side by side during a famous charge that was debated by the Spaniards and later by the Democrats.

After the war, Remsen returned to his usual life. 
One day, O’Roon showed up at his club. 
They greeted each other like old friends, even though O’Roon looked down on his luck.

“Can you get me a job, Remsen?” O’Roon asked. “I just spent my last bit of money.”

“No problem,” said Remsen. “I know people with businesses downtown. Any job you prefer?”

“Yes,” said O’Roon, looking interested. “I walked in Central Park today. I'd like to be one of those mounted policemen. I can ride, and I like the fresh air. Think you can get me that job?”

Remsen was confident he could. 
And soon, O’Roon was a mounted policeman in the park, riding a chestnut horse and doing his duties.

Now, we must mention love at first sight, even if it might bore some people.
It happened as Remsen was walking on Fifth Avenue from his club.

A car was moving slowly because of the traffic. 
Inside was a chauffeur and an old man with white side whiskers and a plaid cap that only someone important would wear. 
But they weren't important—except for driving and paying for the car. 
Beside the old man sat a young lady more beautiful than flowers, more lovely than the moon seen through trees at twilight. 
Remsen saw her and knew he was in love. 
He could have thrown himself under the car for her, but he knew that wouldn't get her attention. 
The car moved on, taking Remsen's heart with it. 
In this big city, he hoped to see her again, believing his love story had its own guardian angel.

Luckily, Remsen was distracted by a reunion of the Gentle Riders. 
There were speeches, food, and drinks, and they talked about their past adventures. 
When it was time to leave, some stayed behind, including O’Roon, who wasn't used to strong drinks.

“I’m drunk, Remsen,” said O’Roon. “Why do hotels spin around like this? They'll take my badge away. I can think and talk, but I can't walk straight. I have to work in three hours. It's over for me, Remsen.”

“Look at me,” said Remsen, smiling. “Who do you see?”

“Good old Remsen,” said O’Roon, dizzy.

“No,” said Remsen. “You see Mounted Policeman O’Roon. Look at my face and think of yours. We look alike. I'll wear your badge and uniform and do your job in the park today. It'll be a great adventure for me.”

On time, Remsen, pretending to be Policeman O’Roon, rode into the park on the chestnut horse. 
In uniform, two men who look a bit alike can seem like twins. 
So Remsen enjoyed his day, as few rich men have such fun.

In the morning, a carriage with two lively horses came down the drive. 
It was unusual because the park is usually quiet in the morning. 
Inside was the old man with the plaid cap and the lady Remsen loved—the one who looked like flowers and the moon.

Remsen met them as they passed. 
Her eyes met his, and he thought she blushed a little. 
He rode on, then turned when he heard the horses running away. 
The horses had bolted.

Remsen chased after them. 
The chestnut horse ran alongside the runaway horses, and Remsen managed to grab the reins and stop them. 
The driver jumped down to hold the horses. 
Remsen was aware of the old man talking nonstop and the lady's eyes, which seemed to thank him. 
They asked his name and thanked him for his bravery, but the lady's eyes were what mattered.

Remsen felt proud because he had a good name and fortune. 
He opened his mouth to speak but closed it again.

Who was he? Mounted Policeman O’Roon. 
He had O’Roon's badge and honor in his hands. 
If he revealed his true identity, O’Roon would be in trouble. 
Love had come, but friendship and loyalty came first.

Remsen touched his cap and spoke plainly.

“Don’t mention it,” he said. “We policemen are paid to do these things. It’s our duty.”

And he rode away, knowing he couldn't have done anything else.
At the end of the day, Remsen returned the horse and went to O’Roon’s room. 
O’Roon was back to his usual self, smoking a cigar.

“I wish you and the police force were at the devil,” said Remsen, feelingly.

O’Roon smiled.

“Good old Remsen,” he said. “I know all about it. They found me here two hours ago. I had a little trouble at home and left to show them. I didn’t tell you my father is the Earl of Ardsley. Funny you met them in the park. If you hurt my horse, I’ll never forgive you. I’m going to buy him. Oh, and my sister—Lady Angela—wants you to come to the hotel with me tonight. You didn’t lose my badge, did you? I need to return it when I resign.”
