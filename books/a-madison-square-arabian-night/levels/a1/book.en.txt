[[CHAPTER: Chapter 1]]

A MADISON SQUARE ARABIAN NIGHT

Carson Chalmers is in his apartment.

Phillips brings the evening mail.

There are two letters with the same foreign stamp.

One letter has a photo of a woman.

The other letter has hurtful words about the woman.

Chalmers tears the letter into pieces.

He walks back and forth on his rug.

He feels like a trapped animal.

He calms down after a while.

The rug is only sixteen feet long.

He needs to go far, but he cannot.

Phillips comes in quietly.

“Will you eat here or out?” he asks.

“Here,” says Chalmers, “in half an hour.”

He listens to the cold wind outside.

“Wait,” he says to Phillips.

“I saw men standing in rows at the square.”

“Why are they there?”

“They are homeless men,” says Phillips.

“A man tries to find them a place to sleep.”

“People give him money for beds.”

“Bring one of those men here for dinner,” says Chalmers.

“Which one?” asks Phillips.

“Choose one,” says Chalmers.

“Make sure he is sober and clean.”

This is not usual for Chalmers.

He wants something different to feel better.

In half an hour, dinner is ready.

Phillips brings a homeless man inside.

The man looks tired and cold.

He is clean but looks out of place.

His face is pale with red stubble.

His hair is messy and long.

His eyes look sad and strong.

His coat is shabby but buttoned high.

Chalmers stands up from his chair.

“Please join me for dinner,” says Chalmers.

“My name is Plumer,” says the man.

“I like to know who I eat with.”

“My name is Chalmers,” says Chalmers.

“Please sit here,” he says.

Plumer sits down, and Phillips helps him.

Phillips brings anchovies and olives.

“Good!” says Plumer. “Dinner in courses, is it?”

“I’m your storyteller tonight,” he says.

“I was forty-third in line,” he says.

“I had little chance for a bed.”

“How do you want my story?” he asks.

“Do you want it with each course?”

“Or with cigars and coffee?”

“This is not new to you,” says Chalmers.

“No!” says Plumer. “New York has many like you.”

“I’ve told my story for food before.”

“No one gives something for nothing here.”

“They want your life story with the meal.”

“I don’t ask your story,” says Chalmers.

“I just wanted company for dinner.”

“You won’t suffer my curiosity.”

“Oh, nonsense!” says Plumer, enjoying his soup.

“I don’t mind telling stories for food.”

“I’m ready with stories when needed.”

“We have a rate for this,” he says.

“For a sandwich, I say drink did it.”

“For corned beef, I tell a landlord story.”

“For steak, I tell a Wall Street story.”

“This is the first fancy meal I’ve had.”

“I’ll tell you the truth if you listen.”

“It’s harder to believe than made-up ones.”

An hour later, Plumer finishes his meal.

Phillips brings coffee and cigars.

“Did you hear of Sherrard Plumer?” he asks.

“I remember the name,” says Chalmers.

“He was a painter, right?”

“Five years ago,” says Plumer. “I fell hard.”

“I’m Sherrard Plumer,” he says.

“I sold my last portrait for $2,000.”

“After that, no one wanted my work.”

“What happened?” asks Chalmers.

“I don’t know,” says Plumer. “It’s strange.”

“I was doing well for a while.”

“I got into high society and had many jobs.”

“Then things changed,” he says.

“I painted what I saw, but it ruined me.”

“Some people were angry and refused their pictures.”

“I painted a woman, and her husband divorced her.”

“A banker sat for me, and a friend saw the portrait.”

“He moved his money after seeing it.”

“I was soon out of work.”

“People don’t want their secrets shown.”

“I couldn’t get another job.”

“I turned to drinking for comfort.”

“I was in the free-bed line, telling stories.”

“Does the truth bore you?” he asks.

“No, no,” says Chalmers. “You interest me.”

“Did all your portraits show something bad?”

“Some were good,” says Plumer.

“Children, many women, and some men.”

“Not everyone is bad,” he says.

Chalmers has a photograph on his table.

He asks Plumer to make a pastel sketch.

After an hour, Plumer finishes the sketch.

“It’s done,” he says, yawning.

“I’m tired,” he says. “No bed last night.”

“Good night, O Commander of the Faithful!”

Chalmers gives him money.

“I’ll take it,” says Plumer.

“Thanks for the good dinner.”

“I’ll sleep well tonight.”

Chalmers paces on his rug again.

He stays away from the sketch.

He rings for Phillips.

“There is a young artist here,” he says.

“A Mr. Reineman—do you know him?”

“Top floor, front,” says Phillips.

“Ask him to come here.”

Reineman comes quickly.

Chalmers introduces himself.

“There’s a pastel sketch on that table.”

“I’d like your opinion on it.”

Reineman looks at the sketch.

“How do you find it?” asks Chalmers.

“It’s the work of a master,” says Reineman.

“The face, man—the subject—what do you say?”

“The face is an angel’s face,” says Reineman.

“May I ask who—”

“My wife!” shouts Chalmers.

“She is traveling in Europe.”

“Paint the picture of your life from it.”
