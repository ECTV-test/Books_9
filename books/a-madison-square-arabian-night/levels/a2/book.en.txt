[[CHAPTER: Chapter 1]]

A MADISON SQUARE ARABIAN NIGHT

Carson Chalmers was in his apartment near the square.
Phillips brought the evening mail.
There were two items with the same foreign postmark.

One parcel had a photo of a woman.
The other had a long letter from another woman.
The letter was full of hidden insults about the woman in the photo.

Chalmers tore the letter into tiny pieces.
He walked back and forth on his expensive rug.
He felt like a trapped animal, full of doubt.

He calmed down after a while.
The rug was only sixteen feet long, not three thousand miles.

Phillips appeared, as he always did, like a well-oiled genie.

“Will you dine here, sir, or out?” he asked.

“Here,” said Chalmers, “and in half an hour.”
He listened to the cold January wind blowing through the empty street.

“Wait,” he said to Phillips.
“As I came home, I saw many men standing in rows.
One man was talking to them.
Why are they there?”

“They are homeless men, sir,” said Phillips.
“The man tries to get them a place to sleep for the night.
People give him money, and he sends as many as he can to a lodging-house.”

“By the time dinner is served,” said Chalmers, “have one of those men here to dine with me.”

“W-w-which—,” began Phillips, stammering for the first time.

“Choose one at random,” said Chalmers.
“Make sure he is sober and somewhat clean.
That is all.”

It was unusual for Carson Chalmers to act like a Caliph.
But that night, he needed something exciting and different to lift his mood.

In half an hour, Phillips had finished his duties.
The waiters brought up the delicious dinner.
The dining table, set for two, glowed warmly with pink candles.

Phillips brought in the shivering guest from the line of homeless men.

The man looked like a wreck, like a ship damaged by fire.
His face and hands were freshly washed, as Phillips insisted.
In the candlelight, he stood out in the neat apartment.
His face was pale, with stubble the color of a red dog's coat.
Phillips's comb couldn't tame his long, messy hair.
His eyes showed hopeless defiance.
His shabby coat was buttoned high, but a bit of collar showed.
He wasn't embarrassed when Chalmers stood up.

“If you will oblige me,” said Chalmers, “I will be glad to have your company at dinner.”

“My name is Plumer,” said the guest, in a harsh voice.
“If you’re like me, you like to know the name of the person you’re dining with.”

“I was going to say,” continued Chalmers quickly, “that mine is Chalmers. Will you sit opposite?”

Plumer sat down as Phillips slid the chair under him.
He seemed used to dining at attended tables.
Phillips set out the anchovies and olives.

“Good!” said Plumer; “going to be in courses, is it? All right, my jovial ruler of Bagdad.
I’m your Scheherezade all the way to the toothpicks.
You’re the first Caliph with a genuine Oriental flavor I’ve met since frost.
What luck! And I was forty-third in line.
I finished counting just as your man arrived to invite me to the feast.
I had little chance of getting a bed tonight.
How will you have the sad story of my life, Mr. Al Raschid—a chapter with each course or the whole edition with the cigars and coffee?”

“The situation does not seem new to you,” said Chalmers with a smile.

“By the chin whiskers of the prophet—no!” answered Plumer.
“New York’s full of cheap Haroun al Raschids.
I’ve been asked for my story with a meal twenty times.
Nobody in New York gives you something for nothing!
They spell curiosity and charity the same way.
Lots of them will give you a dime and chop-suey; a few will play Caliph with a steak; but they all want your story.
I know what to do when I see food coming in Bagdad-on-the-Subway.
I bow and get ready to tell stories for my supper.
I claim descent from Tommy Tucker, who sang for his supper.”

“I do not ask your story,” said Chalmers.
“It was a sudden whim to invite a stranger to dine with me.
You will not suffer from my curiosity.”

“Oh, fudge!” said the guest, enjoying his soup.
“I don’t mind it.
I’m a regular Oriental magazine with a red cover when the Caliph walks abroad.
We have a sort of union rate for this.
Someone always wants to know what brought us down.
For a sandwich and beer, I say drink did it.
For corned beef and coffee, I tell the hard-hearted-landlord story.
A steak and a bed gets the Wall Street tragedy.
This is the first spread like this I’ve had.
I’ll tell you the truth for this, if you’ll listen.
It’s harder to believe than the made-up ones.”

An hour later, the guest relaxed as Phillips brought coffee and cigars.

“Did you ever hear of Sherrard Plumer?” he asked, smiling strangely.

“I remember the name,” said Chalmers.
“He was a painter, I think, of some fame a few years ago.”

“Five years,” said the guest.
“Then I fell like a stone.
I’m Sherrard Plumer!
I sold my last portrait for $2,000.
After that, no one wanted a free picture.”

“What was the trouble?” Chalmers asked.

“Funny thing,” answered Plumer, grimly.
“I never quite understood it.
For a while, I was popular.
I broke into high society and got many commissions.
The newspapers called me a fashionable painter.
Then strange things happened.
When I finished a picture, people would whisper and look at each other.”

“I soon found out the trouble.
I had a knack for showing the hidden character in a portrait.
I don’t know how I did it—I painted what I saw—but it ruined me.
Some sitters were angry and refused their pictures.
I painted a beautiful society woman.
Her husband looked at it and soon sued for divorce.”

“I remember a banker who sat for me.
While his portrait was in my studio, an acquaintance saw it.
‘Does he really look like that?’ he asked.
I said it was a faithful likeness.
‘I never noticed that expression before,’ he said; ‘I think I’ll change my bank account.’
He did, but the bank account was gone, and so was the banker.”

“It wasn’t long before I was out of business.
People don’t want their hidden faults shown in a picture.
They can smile and deceive you, but the picture can’t.
I couldn’t get another order and had to give up.
I worked as a newspaper artist and for a lithographer, but had the same trouble.
If I drew from a photo, my drawing showed traits not in the photo, but probably in the original.
Customers complained, especially women, and I couldn’t keep a job.
So I turned to drinking for comfort.
Soon I was homeless, telling stories for food.
Does the truth bore you, O Caliph?
I can tell the Wall Street disaster story if you prefer, but it needs a tear, and I can’t manage one after that good dinner.”

“No, no,” said Chalmers, earnestly, “you interest me very much.
Did all of your portraits reveal something unpleasant, or were some fine?”

“Some were fine,” said Plumer.
“Children, many women, and enough men.
Not everyone is bad.
When they were good, the pictures were good.
I don’t explain it, but I’m telling you facts.”

On Chalmers’s table lay the photograph he received that day.
Ten minutes later, Plumer was making a sketch from it in pastels.
After an hour, the artist stretched wearily.

“It’s done,” he yawned.
“Excuse me for taking so long.
I got interested in the job.
I’m tired.
No bed last night, you know.
Guess it’s good night now, O Commander of the Faithful!”

Chalmers walked with him to the door and slipped some bills into his hand.

“Oh! I’ll take them,” said Plumer.
“That’s included in the fall.
Thanks for the very good dinner.
I shall sleep well tonight and dream of Bagdad.
I hope it won’t be just a dream in the morning.
Farewell, most excellent Caliph!”


[[CHAPTER: Chapter 2]]

Chalmers walked back and forth on his rug. He stayed away from the table with the pastel sketch.

He tried to go near it a few times but couldn't. He saw the colors, but his fears stopped him.

He sat down to calm himself, then jumped up and called for Phillips.

"There is a young artist in this building," he said. "A Mr. Reineman—do you know his apartment?"

"Top floor, front, sir," said Phillips.

"Go up and ask him to come here for a few minutes."

Reineman came quickly. Chalmers introduced himself.

"Mr. Reineman," he said, "there is a pastel sketch on that table. I would like your opinion on its artistic value and as a picture."

The young artist went to the table and picked up the sketch. Chalmers turned away, leaning on a chair.

"How do you find it?" he asked, slowly.

"As a drawing," said the artist, "I can't praise it enough. It's the work of a master—bold, fine, and true. It puzzles me a little; I haven't seen pastel work this good in years."

"The face, man—the subject—the original—what would you say of that?"

"The face," said Reineman, "is the face of one of God's own angels. May I ask who—"

"My wife!" shouted Chalmers, turning and grabbing the surprised artist, shaking his hand and patting his back. "She is traveling in Europe. Take that sketch and paint the picture of your life from it and leave the price to me."
