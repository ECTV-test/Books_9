TWO THANKSGIVING DAY GENTLEMEN

There is one day that belongs to us. It's the day when all Americans who are not self-made go back home. We eat biscuits and notice how the old pump seems closer to the porch than before. We love this day. President Roosevelt gives it to us. We hear about the Puritans, but we don't really remember who they were. We think we could beat them if they came back. Plymouth Rocks? That sounds more familiar. Many of us have had to eat chicken since the Turkey Trust became powerful. But someone in Washington is telling them about these Thanksgiving plans early.

The big city east of the cranberry bogs has made Thanksgiving Day important. The last Thursday in November is the only day it thinks about the part of America across the ferries. It is a day of celebration, only for Americans.

Now, here is a story to show you that we have traditions here that are getting older faster than those in England, thanks to our energy and drive.

Stuffy Pete sat on the third bench to the right as you enter Union Square from the east, opposite the fountain. Every Thanksgiving Day for nine years, he sat there at 1 o'clock. Every time he did, things happened to him—like in a Charles Dickens story, making his waistcoat swell.

But today, Stuffy Pete was there more out of habit than because he was hungry. He had just eaten a big meal that left him barely able to breathe or move. His eyes looked like pale gooseberries in a swollen, gravy-covered face. He breathed in short wheezes. His coat collar was pushed up by his fat neck. Buttons sewn on by kind Salvation Army hands a week ago popped off, landing around him. He was ragged, with a torn shirt front open to the cold air, but the November breeze with snowflakes felt good to him. Stuffy Pete was full from a big dinner that started with oysters and ended with plum pudding, including all the turkey, potatoes, chicken salad, pie, and ice cream he could eat. So he sat, stuffed, and looked at the world with after-dinner satisfaction.

The meal was a surprise. He was walking past a red brick mansion on Fifth Avenue, where two old ladies lived. They loved traditions and didn't believe New York existed. They thought Thanksgiving was only for Washington Square. One of their traditions was to have a servant let in the first hungry person who came by after noon and give him a big meal. Stuffy Pete passed by on his way to the park, and they brought him in and kept their tradition.

After looking straight ahead for ten minutes, Stuffy wanted to see something else. With effort, he turned his head to the left. Then his eyes widened in fear, and he stopped breathing. His legs moved on the gravel.

The Old Gentleman was coming across Fourth Avenue toward his bench.

Every Thanksgiving Day for nine years, the Old Gentleman had found Stuffy Pete on his bench. He was trying to make it a tradition. Every year he took Stuffy to a restaurant and watched him eat a big dinner. They do these things in England without thinking. But this is a young country, and nine years isn't bad. The Old Gentleman was a true American and thought of himself as starting an American tradition. To become interesting, we must keep doing one thing for a long time without stopping. Like collecting weekly dimes in insurance or cleaning the streets.

The Old Gentleman walked straight and proud toward the tradition he was building. Feeding Stuffy Pete every year wasn't a national thing like the Magna Charta or breakfast jam in England. But it was a start. It showed that a Custom wasn't impossible in America.

The Old Gentleman was thin, tall, and sixty. He wore black clothes and old-fashioned glasses that didn't stay on his nose. His hair was whiter and thinner than last year, and he used his big cane more.

As his kind benefactor came up, Stuffy wheezed and shook like a fat dog when a street dog comes near. He wanted to run, but he couldn't get off the bench. The two old ladies' servants had done their job well.

"Good morning," said the Old Gentleman. "I'm glad to see you're well and moving in the beautiful world. For that alone, this day of thanksgiving is well proclaimed. If you come with me, I'll give you a dinner to match your health with your mind."

That's what the Old Gentleman said every time. Every Thanksgiving Day for nine years. The words almost became a tradition. They were like music to Stuffy's ears. But now he looked up at the Old Gentleman with tears of pain. The snow almost sizzled on his sweaty forehead. But the Old Gentleman shivered a little and turned his back to the wind.

Stuffy always wondered why the Old Gentleman spoke sadly. He didn't know it was because he wished he had a son to continue the tradition. A son who would come after him and say, "In memory of my father." Then it would be a real tradition.

But the Old Gentleman had no family. He lived in rented rooms in an old brownstone mansion in a quiet street east of the park. In winter, he grew flowers in a small conservatory. In spring, he walked in the Easter parade. In summer, he stayed at a farmhouse in New Jersey and talked about a butterfly he hoped to find. In autumn, he fed Stuffy a dinner. These were the Old Gentleman's activities.

Stuffy Pete looked up at him for a half-minute, feeling sorry for himself. The Old Gentleman's eyes were bright with the joy of giving. His face had more lines each year, but his little black tie was as neat as ever, and his shirt was clean and white. His gray mustache was carefully curled. Then Stuffy made a noise like peas boiling in a pot. He meant to speak, and the Old Gentleman understood it as Stuffy's usual acceptance.

"Thank you, sir. I'll go with you, and I'm very grateful. I'm very hungry, sir."

Even though Stuffy was full, he knew he was part of a tradition. His Thanksgiving appetite wasn't his own; it belonged to this kind old gentleman. America is free, but to make a tradition, someone must repeat it. Not all heroes are made of steel and gold. Some use only simple tools.

The Old Gentleman led Stuffy to the restaurant and the table where they always ate. They were recognized.

"Here comes the old guy," said a waiter, "who treats that same bum to a meal every Thanksgiving."

The Old Gentleman sat across the table, happy with his part in a future tradition. The waiters filled the table with holiday food, and Stuffy, with a sigh mistaken for hunger, started eating.

No hero ever fought harder. Turkey, chops, soups, vegetables, pies disappeared as fast as they were served. Stuffy was already full when he entered the restaurant, but the smell of food almost made him lose his honor. He saw the happy look on the Old Gentleman's face—a happier look than even his flowers and butterflies brought—and he couldn't let it fade.

In an hour, Stuffy leaned back, the battle won. "Thank you kindly, sir," he said, like a leaky steam pipe. "Thank you for a hearty meal." Then he got up heavily with glazed eyes and headed for the kitchen. A waiter turned him around and pointed him to the door. The Old Gentleman counted out $1.30 in change, leaving three nickels for the waiter.

They parted as they did each year at the door, the Old Gentleman going south, Stuffy north.

Around the first corner, Stuffy stopped for a minute. Then he seemed to puff out his rags like an owl and fell to the sidewalk like a horse in the sun.

When the ambulance came, the young doctor and driver cursed softly at his weight. There was no smell of alcohol, so Stuffy and his two dinners went to the hospital. They put him on a bed and started checking him for strange diseases.

And an hour later, another ambulance brought the Old Gentleman. They put him on another bed and talked about appendicitis, thinking he was a good case.

But soon, one of the young doctors met a young nurse he liked and stopped to talk about the cases.

"That nice old gentleman over there," he said, "you wouldn't think he was almost starving. Proud old family, I guess. He told me he hadn't eaten for three days."
